## Introduction

This project was undertaken as part of the CSE0001 Digital Literacy course at VIT Bhopal University. The scenario placed me in the role of a Student Digital Ambassador — someone responsible for helping peers navigate the digital world safely, professionally, and productively. Over the course of five tasks, I explored the breadth of digital literacy: from creating visual awareness resources and building a professional online portfolio, to practising on coding platforms, drafting formal emails, and researching real cybercrime threats facing Indian students today. Every task pushed me to move beyond passive consumption of digital tools and think critically about how I use them. This report documents my process, observations, and reflections for each task, and captures the key lessons I will carry forward as a digitally literate student and future professional.

---

## Task 1 — Digital Literacy Awareness Infographic

For Task 1, I created a single-page infographic using Canva, a free browser-based graphic design platform. The infographic was designed to serve as an awareness resource that I, as a Digital Ambassador, could share with my batchmates to explain what digital literacy really means and why it matters for first-year students.

The infographic covers three core topics: first, a concise definition of digital literacy — explaining that it is not just about using computers, but about finding, evaluating, creating, and communicating information responsibly using digital tools. Second, a curated list of useful digital tools for students, highlighting platforms like Google Workspace for collaboration, GitHub for code management, LinkedIn for professional networking, and HackerRank for coding practice. Third, a section on safe internet practices, including tips on creating strong passwords, recognising phishing attempts, and being mindful of one's digital footprint.

The most interesting part of creating this infographic was realising how much thought goes into visual communication. Choosing the right layout, icons, and colour scheme to make the content scannable — not just readable — was a skill I had not practised before. Canva's template library and drag-and-drop editor made the process accessible, but the challenge was in deciding what to include and what to leave out within the constraint of a single page.

## Task 2 — Student Digital Portfolio

For Task 2, I set up professional profiles on three platforms: GitHub, LinkedIn, and Kaggle. Each platform serves a distinct purpose in a student's digital portfolio, and the task helped me understand why building an online presence early is essential.

On **GitHub**, I created a profile README — a special repository named after my username — that includes my name, branch (B.Tech AI), year, and a sentence about my goal of learning machine learning and building impactful AI applications. GitHub is where all my coding projects will live, and this repository itself is hosted there. On **LinkedIn**, I completed my Education section with my degree, institution, and expected graduation year. LinkedIn is the platform recruiters and internship coordinators use to find candidates, so having a well-structured profile ready from the first year gives me a head start. On **Kaggle**, I set up my account and bio, positioning it as a platform where I can practise data science skills through competitions and shared datasets.

What struck me most is that setting up these profiles took less than an hour, yet the long-term value they offer is immense. Having a digital presence that grows alongside my academic journey means that by the time I apply for internships in my third year, I will already have a track record rather than starting from scratch. I plan to keep all three profiles active — committing code to GitHub weekly, engaging with content on LinkedIn, and attempting at least one Kaggle competition per semester.

## Task 3 — Coding & Collaboration Platforms

In Task 3, I explored both coding practice platforms and cloud collaboration tools — two essential pillars of the modern digital workspace.

For the coding practice component, I registered on **HackerRank** and completed the "Solve Me First" challenge, a beginner-level problem that requires reading two integers and printing their sum. While the problem itself was straightforward, the value lay in learning how the platform works: the web-based code editor, the automated test case validation system, and the scoring/badge mechanism. It demystified what competitive programming environments look like and encouraged me to continue practising on the platform beyond this assignment.

For the collaboration component, I created a **Google Form** titled "Digital Literacy Awareness Quiz" with five questions designed to test my batchmates' understanding of key digital literacy concepts. The quiz included a mix of multiple-choice and short-answer questions covering topics such as identifying phishing, understanding digital footprint, and recognising strong passwords. Linking the form to a Google Sheet and watching responses auto-populate gave me a practical understanding of how cloud-based collaboration tools work — something that will be directly useful for group projects, event registrations, and feedback collection throughout my college life.

The most valuable takeaway from this task was seeing how accessible these platforms are. Between HackerRank for sharpening technical skills and Google Workspace for everyday collaboration, I now have a concrete toolkit I can recommend to my peers as a Digital Ambassador.

## Task 4 — Professional Email & Communication

Task 4 focused on professional email writing and responsible social media use — two aspects of digital communication that are often overlooked by students but carry significant real-world consequences.

I drafted two emails: one requesting a deadline extension from a professor, and another expressing interest in a summer internship. Writing these helped me internalise the structure of a professional email — clear subject line, respectful greeting, concise body with a specific ask, and a proper sign-off. The key learning was that brevity and clarity matter far more than length; a short, well-structured email conveys professionalism, while a long, rambling one does the opposite.

One scenario that illustrates the cost of poor digital communication is when students accidentally "Reply All" to an entire batch email list with a casual or inappropriate message. What seems like a minor mistake can damage one's reputation with faculty and peers instantly. The lesson is simple: re-read every email before sending, double-check the recipient field, and always maintain a professional tone in academic and workplace communication.

## Task 5 — Cybercrime Awareness

For Task 5, I researched **UPI/Online Payment Fraud** — a cybercrime that is alarmingly common in India yet poorly understood by most college students.

What surprised me most during my research was how simple and low-tech these scams actually are. Attackers do not need sophisticated hacking skills; they exploit a basic misunderstanding of how UPI collect requests work. A scammer simply sends a collect request and convinces the victim to "accept" it under the guise of receiving a payment. The fact that the RBI reported a 70% rise in UPI fraud complaints between 2022 and 2024 shows this is not a niche problem — it is a systemic gap in digital financial literacy.

The one habit I will personally change as a result of this task is how I handle incoming UPI requests. Previously, I did not pay close attention to whether a notification was a "pay" or "collect" request — I just assumed incoming notifications meant I was receiving money. Now, I will carefully read every UPI notification before taking any action, and I will never enter my PIN for a collect request from an unknown person. I have also saved the Cyber Crime Helpline number (1930) on my phone, and I will encourage my friends and family to do the same.

## Conclusion

This project transformed digital literacy from an abstract concept into a set of concrete, actionable skills. Through creating an infographic, building professional profiles, exploring coding platforms, writing formal emails, and researching UPI fraud, I gained a well-rounded understanding of how to navigate the digital world both safely and productively. The most important lesson I take away is that digital literacy is not a one-time learning — it is an ongoing practice of staying informed, staying cautious, and continuously building one's professional presence online. As a Student Digital Ambassador, I now feel better equipped to guide my peers through these same journeys.
